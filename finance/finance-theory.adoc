Corporations invest in real assets, **these generate income**. Some of the assets
can be:

Tangible: Plant, machinery

Intangible: Brand names, patents

Corporations finance their **investments** by _borrowing_, _retaining_, and
_reinvesting cash flow_, **AND** selling additional shares of stock to the
corporation's shareholders. So, financial managers are always faced with two
questions:

- What investments should the corporation make?
- How sohuld it pay for those investments?

Investment decision involves **spending money** and financing decision means
**raising money**

The biggest problem is "how do you raise value? (of the corporation)". Because
shareholders can be hundreds of thousands. Their profiles can differ in wealth,
risk tolerance, investment horizon. But the secret is to go for the clearest and
most in the middle choice that satisfies all of them. Make them wealthier, so
that then **they take decisions on their own on how to use that wealth**

There may be a few activities in which one can read a textbook and then just "do it",
but financial management is not one of them. That is why finance is worth studying.
Who wants to work in a field where there is no room for judgement, experience,
creativity and a pinch of luck?

_A book can't guarantee any of those things, but it can try to cover the concepts
that govern good financial decisions, and show you how to use the tools of the
trade of modern finance_

Financial managers, refer to the _opportunity cost_ of the capital contributed
by shareholders. Whatever they could earn **outside the company** sets the standard
for investments _inside_ the corporation.

And managers, of course, are human beings with their own interests and circumstances;
they are not always the perfect servants of shareholders. Therefore corporations
must combine governance rules and procedures with appropriate incentives to make
sure that all managers and employees-not just the financial managers-pull together
to increase value.

Shareholders do not want the maximum possible stock price. They want the maximum
honest stock price. Good governance plus appropriate incentives should help block
out temptations to increase stock price by illegal or unethical means.

The five themes that occur again and again throughout the book are:

1. Corporate finance is all about maximizing value
2. The opportunity cost of capital sets the standard for investment decisions
3. A safe dollar is worth more than a risky dollar
4. Smart investment decisions create more value than smart financing decisions
5. Good governance matters

To carry on business, a corporation needs an almost endless variety of **real
assets**. They have to **pay for them**. The corporation pays for its real assets
by selling claims on them **and** on the cash flow that they will generate. These
claims are called **financial assets or securities**

- Bank loan => Financial Asset **ONLY**. It's a promise to pay the loan that the
corporation gives to the bank. CAN'T BE TRADED IN FINANCIAL MARKETS
- Corporate bond => This is sold to investors, _also a promise_ but to pay interest
on the bond and pay off the bond at its maturity. **Financial asset PLUS security**

Securities include:

- Bonds
- Shares of stock
- And a dizzying variety of specialized instruments

This suggests the following definitions:

Investment decision = purchase of real assets
Financing decision = sale of securities and other financial assets

_Note:_ You aren't "selling financial assets" unless you are a Bank. When they
take the promises of whoever (bundle of thousands of mortgages or auto loans),
and they sell them to investors. That can be indeed referred to as "selling financial
assets". _However, in everyday speech what you sell, mostly is securities,
"issuing stock", "issuing bonds"_. You would normally say, _"taking out a loan"_

However life is not so simple. Part of the investment decision is to also manage
assets already in place and deciding when to shut down and dispose of assets when
they are no longer profitable. The corporation also has to manage and control
the risks of its investments. _(And don't forget about management and control of
the risks your investments hold)_

Financing, besides raising cash today, **also** means meeting the obligations to
banks, bondholders, stockholders that have contributed financing in the past. For
example, the corporation has to repay its debts when they become due. If it cannot
do so, it ends up insolvent and bankrupt. Sooner or later the corporation will also
want to pay out cash to its shareholders.

**Investment decisions** are often referred to as **capital budgeting** or **
capital expenditure** (**CAPEX**) decisions because most large corporations prepare
an annual **capital budget** listing the major projects approved for investment.

_Some_ investments involve the purchase of tangible assets-assets that you can touch
and kick. However, corporations also need to invest in intangible assets, such as
research and development (R&D), advertising, and computer software. For example,
GlaxoSmithKline and other major pharmaceutical companies invest billions every
year on R&D for new drugs. Similarly, consumer good companies such as Procter &
Gamble invest huge sums in advertising and marketing their products. These outlays
are investments because they build know-how, brand recognition, and reputation
for the long run.

Today's capital investments generate future cash returns. Sometimes the cash inflows
last for decades. For example, many U.S. nuclear power plants, which were initially
licensed by the Nuclear Regulatory Comission to operate for 40 years, are now being
re-licensed for 20 more years and may be able to operate efficiently for 80 years
overall.

_Not all investments have distant payoffs, sometimes investments come within months_

_Cash returns are not guaranteed_. An investment could be a smashing success or a
dismal failure.

_**Side note**_ And sometimes a bad investment decision could end up in bankrupcy.
But even then, private investors can buy the company, and turn it completely around
by taking a stab at it with different investment decisions.

**Do not think that the financial manager makes large investments on a daily basis**.
Most investment decisions are smaller and simpler, such as the purchase of a truck,
machine tool, or computer system. The cumulative amount of small investments can be
just as large as that of the occasional big investments.

_Managers do not make major investment decisions in solitary confinement_. They
may work as part of a team of engineers and managers from manufacturing, marketing,
and other business functions.

=== Financing Decisions

A corporation can raise money from lenders or from shareholders.

If it **borrows**, the lenders contribute the cash, and the corporation promises
to pay back the debt plus a fixed rate of interest.
If the shareholders put up the cash, they do not get a fixed return, but they hold
shares of stock and therefore get a fraction of future profits and cash flow.

The shareholders are _equity investors_, who contribute _equity financing_. The choice
between debt and equity financing is called the **capital structure** decision.
_Capital_ refers to the firm's sources of long-term financing.

**Investor:** Anyone who puts money into something expecting a return.
E.g.,

- Someone who buys P&G stock = investor + shareholder
- A bank that lends money to P&G = investor/creditor, but not shareholder

- Dividends and buybacks: Distribute excess cash to shareholders

````
The shareholders are _equity investors_, who contribute _equity financing_. The
choice between debt and equity financing is called the **capital structure**
decision. _Capital_ refers to the firm's sources of long-term financing.
````

**Equity:** What's left that truly belongs to the owners after all debts are paid.

When finance people say:

- "raise equity"
they mean:
- "sell ownership"

When they say:

- "equity financing"
they mean:
- "funding the business by selling ownership shares instead of borrowing"

**Why stock is called equity**

- A stock share represents:

	- A claim on future profits
	- A claim on residual assets
	- Voting/control rights
		- So, stocks are called equities

Example:
If you own shares of Apple:
	- You are an equity holder
	- Because you own part of the residual value of the company

Some slices are already promised away:

What remains belongs to the owners

The remaining portion is:

_Getting back on track with the book_

The financing choices available to large corporations seem almost endless. Suppose
the firm decides to borrow. Should it borrow from a bank or borrow by issuing
bonds that can be traded by investors? Should it borrow for 1 year or 20 years?
If 20, does it reserve the right to pay off the debt early? Borrow from Paris,
under euro? Or should it borrow dollars in New York?

Corporations raise equity financing in two ways:

- They can issues new shares of stock. The investors who buy the new shares put
up cash in exchange for a fraction of the corporation's future cash flow and profits
- The corporation can take the cash flow generated by its existing assets and reinvest
that cash in new assets. In this case the corporation is reinvesting on behalf of
existing stockholders. No new shares are issued.

**Why is the second option still "equitiy financing"?** => Because all the profit
(net income), does **NOT** belong to the abstract concept of "the company". It
belongs entirely to the owners-the equity shareholders.

At the end of the year the company has two choices for what to do with that
"equity cash":

- **Pay it out:** Give it directly to the shareholders as cash dividends
- **Keep it:** Retain it to buy new assets (reinvesting)

By choosing to keep cash and reinvest it, the company is essentially saying to the
shareholders: "Instead of sending this cash to your bank account today, we are
going to force you to reinvest it back into the business"

_Because it is the **shareholders' money** being put to work, it is classified as
equity financing.

What happens when a corporation does not reinvest all of the cash flow generated
by its existing assets? It may hold the cash in reserve for future investments,
or it may pay the cash back to its shareholders. E.g., Proter & Gamble paid back
$4.6 billion to its stockholders by repurchasing shares. This was in addition to
$7.2 billion paid out as cash dividends.

**Why share repurchases (often called **buybacks**) are considered "paying back
stockholders"?**

_The corporation is literally handing billions of dollars in cash to the stockholders
who choose to sell their shares_

A corporation can be thought of as a giant pie. If the company wants to distribute
cash via a share repurchase, it goes into the stock market and says, "We have $4.6
billion in cash. Who wants to trade their piece of the pie for some of this cash?"

Both the stockholders who **SELL** earn something and the stockholders who **HOLD**

- SELL: If you own Procter & Gamble stock, you hand over your shares and P&G hands you
cash. You officially have been "paid back" your investment capital plus any gains.
- HOLD: When a company buys back its own shares, it **destroys** those shares (takes
them out of circulation). Suddenly, there are fewer total shares in existence.

Because there are fewer shares, **your remaining shares instantly become worth a
larger percentage of the company**

_Often, when you sell, you get taxed on the cash you will get_.

_One reason as to do a buyback:_ You send a signal to the market. The corporation
basically is saying "we believe that investing in our own stock is the best return
of capital we can find". Outside investors start buying that stock too, which drives
the price UP.

_Let's look at a Microsoft example:_

Financial managers tend to say that "value comes mainly from the asset side of the
balance sheet". **The most successful corporations sometimes have the simplest
financing strategies. In December 2017, Microsoft shares traded for about $88 each.
There were 7.7 billion shares outstanding. Therefore Microsoft's overall market value
-its _market capitalization_ or _market cap_-was $88 x 7.7 = $680 billion. Where did
this market value come from? It came from Microsoft's product development:
- Brand name
- Worldwide customer base
- Research and development
- Ability to make profitable future investments

The financing strategy is very simple:

- It carries no debt so to speak and finances almost all investment by retaining
and reinvesting cash flow

Financing decisions may not add much value, compared with good investment decisions,
but they can destroy value if they are stupid or if they are ambushed by bad news.
For example, after a consortium of investment companies bought the energy giant
TXU in 2007, the company took an additional $50 billion of debt. Not _stupid_,
but a nearly fatal decision. Since the value generated by the assets that were
based around natural gas and electricity were hit by shale gas production's rise.
In 2014, the company (renamed Energy Future Holdings) was no longer able to service
its debts and filed for bankruptcy.

==== What is a Corporation?

**Corporation** is a legal entity. In the view of the law, it is a legal _person_
that is owned by its shareholders. As a legal person, the corporation can make
contracts, carry on a business, borrow or lend money, and sue or be sued. One corporation
can make a takeover bid for another and then merge the two businesses. Corporations
pay taxes -BUT CANNOT VOTE!

In the USA, corporations are formed under state law, based on _articles of incorporation_
that set out the purpose of the business and how it is to be governed and operated.
For example, the articles of incorporation specify the composition and role of the
_board of directors_. A corporation's directors are elected by the shareholders.
They choose and advise top management and must sign off on important corporate
actions, such as mergers and the payment of dividends to shareholders.

_NOTE:_ In the US, corporations are identified by the label "Corporation", "Incorporated"
or "Inc.,". The U.K. identifies public corporations by "plc" (Public Limited Corporation).
French corporations have the suffix "SA". The corresponding labels in Germany are
"GmbH" or "AG"
